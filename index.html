Core Concepts

Rate Limiting vs. Request Throttling

Understanding how traffic control strategies protect your infrastructure in different ways.

Rate Limiting Hard Cap

Enforces strict boundaries by capping the total number of requests a user can make within a defined time window (e.g., 100 requests/minute). Once the cap is hit, any extra requests are immediately blocked with a 429 Too Many Requests HTTP status.

  • Ideal for credential stuffing & brute-force protection
  • Used to enforce subscription tiers and monetization quotas
Limit Exceeded: Drop traffic immediately.

Request Throttling Traffic Shaping

Smooths out sudden traffic spikes by queueing and delaying excess requests, letting them leak through to your backend servers at a steady, sustainable rate. Instead of blocking clients outright, it temporarily slows down response times to protect database stability.

  • Ideal for maintaining database sanity during flash sales
  • Avoids user-facing errors by processing bursts gracefully
Limit Exceeded: Queue & slow down response.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between Token Bucket and Leaky Bucket?

Token Bucket allows for short bursts of traffic while maintaining a fixed average rate, whereas Leaky Bucket forces a steady, constant flow of requests, which is stricter but can increase latency for legitimate bursts.

Can you implement rate limiting for GraphQL APIs?

Yes, GraphQL requires cost-based rate limiting rather than simple request counts. I specialize in complexity-based throttling that analyzes query depth and field counts before execution.

How do you handle DDoS attacks at the application layer?

I use a combination of IP reputation scoring, behavioral analysis, and challenge-response (CAPTCHA/Proof of Work) to filter out botnets before they reach your backend services.
